What happened, why it matters, and where to look next
A researcher submitted governance proposals to the Irish State nineteen years ago. They were acknowledged. Nothing was done. Every available oversight channel was tried. Each passed the matter on. None accepted responsibility. This site is the record of that.
§ 01
What Is This?

A public record of a nineteen-year governance failure. Not a blog. Not a protest. A legal and evidential archive — presented here in plain language so that anyone can understand what happened.

Operations Research — one sentence

Operations Research (O.R.) is the science of making better decisions using mathematics and systems analysis. It is used in hospital scheduling, supply chains, military logistics, emergency planning. The Irish State applies it to almost none of its public governance.

What was submitted

Beginning in November 2006, the Meinhardt Initiative formally submitted O.R. frameworks to multiple Irish government departments — Defence, Foreign Affairs, Health, Finance, Emergency Planning — as tools for better public decision-making. The submissions were grounded, documented, and lawfully made.

What "nothing" means

No evaluation. No rejection with reasons. No determination of any kind — neither yes nor no. After nineteen years, six governments, and four Taoisigh, not a single submission has been formally assessed, approved, or refused. That is not delay. It is the systematic nullification of a civic process.

Why this matters beyond one person's complaint

If a state can receive formally submitted public proposals, acknowledge them, and take no action — indefinitely, without legal consequence — then civic participation is not a right. It is a ritual. This site documents that the Irish State has, in practice, treated it as the latter.

§ 02
How It Worked — The Nullification Loop

Every department and government that received the submissions made the same five moves. Together they form what this corpus calls the Nullification Loop — a sequence that produces the same output (no decision) regardless of who is operating it.

Loop invariant
Six Taoisigh. Four governments. Zero determinations.
The output of the loop has been identical across every change of personnel, party, and parliamentary term since 2006. No individual is necessary for the loop to run. The system produces ignoring as its default output.
1
Acknowledgement as Closure
"Thank you, the matter has been noted." The acknowledgement is treated as resolution. No next step follows. Files are sometimes deleted.
2
Remit Displacement
"This does not fall within our remit." The matter is redirected — but to no one in particular. No body ever identifies who does have responsibility.
3
Deferral Without Destination
"This is a matter for policy." No timetable. No named responsible official. Permanent provisionality.
4
Sufficiency Assertion
"Such methodologies are already in use." No evidence provided. No audit. The claim closes the conversation without opening the file.
5
Record Suppression
Correspondence declined, deleted, or not minuted. Institutional memory is deliberately truncated. The record disappears. The loop resets.

§ 04
Why Now

Ireland assumes the Presidency of the Council of the European Union on 1 July 2026. During that Presidency, Ireland will chair deliberations on democratic accountability, rule of law, and good governance across the European Union. The structural irony is documented, timestamped, and archived.

§ 05
Where to Go Next

The corpus is structured in layers — from plain overview to formal legal record. Start where the depth suits you.

The question this whole corpus asks
Can a state acknowledge civic intelligence — formally, repeatedly, over nineteen years — and do nothing with it, without legal or institutional consequence?
If the answer is yes, then acknowledgement discharges obligation. It does not. But in the absence of a determination to the contrary, it functions as if it does. Every document in this archive exists to force a determination.
